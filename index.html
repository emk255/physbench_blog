PhysBench

Elliot Kim
Cornell University

Introduction

We introduce PhysBench, a challenging benchmark designed to assess the physical reasoning capabilities of state-of-the-art large language models (LLMs). PhysBench consists of 843 curated questions across the domains of mechanics, electromagnetism, thermodynamics, optics, and relativity, with 696 problems incorporating images that require multimodal understanding. We find that our benchmark, completely free of data contamination:

  1. (1) is substantially more challenging than existing benchmarks
  2. (2) demonstrates that augmenting a built-in web search tool is adversarial to models' performance.

Experiments

Setup

Submatrix Analysis

To quantify the relative difficulty of our benchmark $B_{\text{Phys}}$ in comparison to existing benchmarks $B_{\text{Existing}}$, we first classify a group of strong models $M_{\text{strong}}$ and weak models $M_{\text{weak}}$ using results from Table 1 with an accuracy rate threshold of $t=0.30$. We then identify sub-matrices of the performance dataset where:

  1. (1) $A(M_{\text{Strong}}, B_{\text{Phys}}) \gg A(M_{\text{Weak}}, B_{\text{Phys}})$, and
  2. (2) $A(M_{\text{Strong}}, B_{\text{Existing}}) \approx A(M_{\text{Weak}}, B_{\text{Existing}})$,

where $A(M,B)$ refers to the average accuracy rate of models in group $M$ on benchmark $B$. In the following group of experiments, $B_{\text{Existing}}=\text{OlympiadBench}$, as it represents the most comparable existing multi-modal physics benchmark in terms of domain coverage and problem difficulty.

Clustering Analysis

We extend submatrix analysis through clustering to discover natural problem groupings beyond predefined features. We generate embeddings directly from problem text (context + question) for all problems across both benchmarks, placing them in a unified vector space. After clustering, we use an LLM to generate keyword lists for problems within each cluster, enabling qualitative interpretation of the discovered subcategories.

Results

Table 2 reveals a substantial discrimination gap between strong and weak models on PhysBench, particularly for problems with short solutions and simple mathematical expressions. This pronounced gap highlights how the benchmark's difficulty arises from conceptual reasoning requirements: success relies on recognizing the relevant knowledge, formula, or framework, rather than merely performing computations. In contrast, OlympiadBench exhibits much less separation between strong and weak models, indicating its problems are more predictable and can often be solved by pattern matching instead of deep understanding.

Clustering problems by embeddings also surfaces a thermodynamics-focused group, where a marked performance gap persists. Analysis of model-generated keywords suggests this cluster covers challenging, real-system physics such as phase transitions and energy transfer.

Case Study: Augmenting Built-in Web Search Tool

Previous studies have revealed that integrating LLMs with web search faces critical challenges including knowledge conflicts, position bias, and tool availability failures. However, this research primarily examined loosely coupled RAG systems using external APIs or custom pipelines. With state-of-the-art LLMs now offering built-in web search tools that combine conversational and information retrieval capabilities, we hypothesize these integrated architectures will improve overall performance.

Results

The table above shows that multi-modal models using built-in web search tools does not improve performance, but rather has an adversarial effect on our benchmark. The magnitude of this effect is greater as the base performance of the model gets worse. This phenomenon stays consistent with prior work on external web-search being integrated with LLMs; this re-emphasizes that our benchmark stresses conceptual inference rather than recall, where additional web evidence often distracts rather than helps.
